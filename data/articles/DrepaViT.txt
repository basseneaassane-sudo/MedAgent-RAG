DrepaViT: An interpretable hybrid CNN–Transformer
model for improved red blood cell classification

Abstract.
We present DrepaViT, a novel hybrid deep learning model for red blood cell
classification in the context of sickle cell disease (SCD) diagnosis. Combining
ResNet-50 and Vision Transformer (ViT) architectures, DrepaViT captures both
local and global morphological features. Trained on the SKDrepaData-v2 dataset—a curated collection of 1,489 annotated microscopy images—our model
achieves state-of-the-art performance with 93.33% accuracy, 93.30% F1-score,
and an AUC of 0.9456. We further enhance interpretability using Grad-CAM,
demonstrating clinically relevant feature localization. A web-based interface supports real-time analysis, improving diagnostic accessibility in low-resource settings. Our results highlight DrepaViT’s potential as a robust, interpretable tool
for automated hematological screening of SCD.
Keywords: Sickle Cell Disease, Red Blood Cell Classification, Vision Transformer, Deep Learning, Explainable AI, Medical Imaging.
1 Introduction
Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a severe genetic disorder caused by a point mutation in
the β-globin gene, resulting in abnormal hemoglobin S (HbS) [1]. Under low oxygen,
HbS polymerizes, deforming red blood cells into a sickle shape and causing vaso-occlusion, anemia, and serious complications [2], [3]. SCD affects over 300,000 newborns
annually in sub-Saharan Africa [4]; in Senegal, 1.9% of newborns present the severe
SS form, and 11.1% of the population carries the HbS allele [5], [6], [7]. Without early
screening, more than half of affected children die before age five.
Traditional diagnosis relies on manual microscopy, limited by subjectivity, cost, and
the need for skilled personnel—resources often lacking in low-income regions [8], [9],
[10]. These constraints hinder early detection, while computer-aided diagnosis (CAD)
systems offer a scalable solution through automated red blood cell classification [11],
[12].
Although convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have achieved strong results in
medical imaging, their local receptive fields limit their ability to model global dependencies—critical in SCD due to the high morphological variability of sickled cells and2 F. Author and S. Author
varying smear conditions[13], [14]. Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as a
promising solution, leveraging self-attention to model long-range spatial relationships
[15]. However, ViTs require large annotated datasets—often impractical in clinical settings due to the cost and scarcity of expert-labelled data. This has driven the development of hybrid architectures that combine CNNs and Transformers to capture both local
and global features for robust SCD detection.
In this study, we introduce DrepaVit, a novel hybrid model that combines ResNet-
50 [16] for local morphological feature extraction with Vision Transformers (ViT) [15]
for global contextual modelling. Designed for enhanced diagnostic accuracy and reduced processing time, DrepaVit addresses the challenges of sickle cell disease (SCD)
detection in resource-limited environments such as sub-Saharan Africa.
Our key contributions are as follows: (1) A dual-branch architecture merging CNN
and Transformer features for robust red blood cell (RBC) classification; (2) A residualnormalized fusion mechanism to combine local and global representations effectively;
(3) A tailored training pipeline using Focal Loss and data augmentation to mitigate class
imbalance; (4) Release of SKDrepaData-v2, a new annotated dataset of blood smear
images from African SCD patients; (5) Comprehensive benchmarking against state-ofthe-art RBC classification models; (6) Integration of Grad-CAM for interpretability and
model transparency; (7) Deployment of an interactive web interface to facilitate clinical
adoption.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work
on SCD classification. Section 3 details the DrepaVit architecture. Section 4 presents
the dataset, experimental setup, and metrics. Section 5 reports and analyses the results.
Section 6 discusses comparisons with state-of-the-art methods. Section 7 concludes
with future directions.
2 Related Work
Historically, sickle cell disease (SCD) diagnosis has relied on manual microscopy, a
process that is time-consuming, expensive, and prone to inter-observer variability [17].
Early AI approaches addressed these limitations through handcrafted feature extraction
combined with classifiers like SVM, KNN, or Random Forest [18], [19], [20]. Though
applied with some success in RBC classification [21], [22], [23], [24], these methods
heavily depend on accurate segmentation and are sensitive to noise and imaging variability.
Deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), has significantly
advanced RBC analysis by learning features directly from images[25]. Xu et al. [14]
achieved 87.5% accuracy and 0.94 AUC using a CNN to classify eight RBC types.
Other efforts have leveraged U-Net [26], transfer learning[27], and benchmarking with
pretrained models [28], [29], [30], [31]. For example, ResNet-50 reached 93.88% accuracy [32], while DAPN-48 achieved 100% [33]. However, CNNs remain limited in
modeling long-range dependencies, which are crucial in detecting subtle morphological
differences in blood cells[34], [35].Contribution Title (shortened if too long) 3
To overcome these limitations, Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged, offering
strong global contextual modeling [36]. Applications in medical imaging—from mammography [37] to dermatology [38]—have shown ViTs often outperform CNNs, especially in multimodal and fine-grained tasks [34], [39], [40]. Yet, their sensitivity to adversarial noise has prompted robust adaptations like SEViT [41].
Hybrid models that integrate CNNs and Transformers combine local feature extraction with global representation learning. Architectures such as PHTrans [42], CTCNet
[43], and MedViT [44] exemplify this synergy. However, in SCD diagnosis, these hybrid models remain underexplored. To bridge this gap, we propose DrepaViT, a novel
CNN-Transformer architecture tailored for precise and interpretable classification of
sickled red blood cells.
3 Proposed DrepaVit Model
DrepaViT is a hybrid architecture that combines ResNet-50 (CNN)[16] and a Vision
Transformer (ViT) [15] to automatically classify red blood cells into three categories—
normal, sickled, and other types—from microscopic images, thereby supporting SCD
diagnosis. The model consists of two parallel branches that process the input image
simultaneously for enhanced robustness:
1. A CNN branch based on ResNet-50 extracts hierarchical local spatial features
at multiple depths.
2. A Transformer branch employs ViT to capture global contextual relationships
via sequential patch processing.
The input image (224×224×3) is fed into both branches. The resulting features are
fused, normalized, and passed through a multilayer perceptron (MLP) classifier, which
outputs class probabilities for the three target categories. Fig. 1 illustrates the complete
DrepaViT architecture.

4 Experiments
4.1 Dataset: SKDrepaData-v2
We conducted our experiments on SKDrepaData-v2 [45], a publicly available dataset
comprising 1,489 Giemsa-stained blood smear images from 20 confirmed SCD patients. Samples were collected from two Senegalese institutions: UMRED (University
of Thiès) and the Children’s Hospital of Diamniadio. Images (224 × 224 × 3) were
denoised using Noise2Void [46] and manually annotated by hematologists into three
classes: normal, sickled, and other abnormal cells. The dataset reflects clinical class
imbalance; distribution details are shown in Fig. 2.
Figure 2: Overview of the dataset showing class distribution and sample cell images
4.2 Class Imbalance and Data Augmentation
Due to the natural imbalance in red blood cell types, two complementary strategies
were adopted to improve model generalization:6 F. Author and S. Author
Data augmentation was applied to the training set using random crops with resizing, horizontal and vertical flips, color jitter, and normalization. These transformations
aimed to increase sample diversity, particularly for underrepresented classes, and to
reduce overfitting.
Weighted sampling was used during training to balance class frequency across
mini-batches by assigning higher weights to minority classes.
The dataset was split into 70% for training (1,131 images), 20% for validation (268
images), and 10% for testing (90 images).
4.3 Training Setup
DrepaViT was trained for 100 epochs using the AdamW optimizer with a CosineAnnealingLR learning rate scheduler. To address class imbalance during training, we used
Focal Loss[47], which down-weights the contribution of well-classified samples and
focuses learning on hard-to-classify examples. Key hyperparameters are summarized
in Table 1.

4.4 Evaluation Metrics
To assess model performance, we used several standard metrics: accuracy, precision,
recall, F1-score, and Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC). AUC was computed from
ROC curves by plotting true positive rate against false positive rate across different
thresholds. Higher AUC values indicate better class separability.

5.2 Model Interpretability and Feature Analysis
To improve interpretability, we used Grad-CAM[48], which highlights key regions influencing predictions. Although this plot is not shown due to space constraints,
DrepaViT consistently localizes discriminative morphological patterns. This results
from the complementary strengths of its hybrid CNN–Transformer branches design,
combining fine-grained features from ResNet-50 with global context modeling from
ViT. Their additive fusion optimally integrates the feature streams.

6 Conclusion and Future Work
In this study, we introduced DrepaViT, a hybrid deep learning model that integrates a
CNN branch (ResNet-50) with a Transformer branch (ViT) for the classification of red
blood cells in the context of sickle cell disease diagnosis. By leveraging both local features and global contextual relationships, our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SKDrepaData-v2 dataset, with an accuracy of 93.33%, F1-score of
93.30%, and AUC of 94.56 on the test set.
Interpretability is ensured through Grad-CAM visualizations, highlighting discriminative regions crucial for clinical validation. An interactive web interface further supports its deployment in low-resource settings.
Future work includes hyperparameter tuning and integration of multimodal data to
enhance the model’s robustness. Overall, DrepaViT offers a promising step toward automated, interpretable, and accessible tool, with strong potential for hematological
