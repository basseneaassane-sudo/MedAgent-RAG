Abstract— The detection of blood cells in patients with sickle
cell disease represents a significant challenge in computer vision,
primarily due to the cells' small size and tendency to overlap. In
this study, we propose DREPADetect, an optimized detection
model for blood cells, based on a modified YOLOv8
architecture. To enhance accuracy for small objects, we
streamlined the model by selectively removing blocks and layers
dedicated to detecting medium and large-sized objects. This
adaptation allows computational resources to focus on the
morphological features specific to blood cells. We optimized the
backbone and neck components of the model by reinforcing
blocks specialized in high-resolution feature extraction, which is
crucial for detecting small objects. This approach enables
DREPADetect to minimize the influence of artifacts and offtarget elements in images, focusing attention on regions of
interest (ROIs) corresponding to blood cells. Experimental
results demonstrate that DREPADetect achieves superior
performance compared to state-of-the-art models, with a
precision of 87.6%, a recall of 87.6%, and a mean average
precision (mAP@50) of 90.3% on a dedicated dataset of blood
cells from sickle cell patients. These results outperform
reference models such as YOLOv8 and derivatives. This study
highlights the effectiveness of DREPADetect for the accurate
detection of blood cells in a complex clinical context, offering
promising prospects for automated medical image analysis in
sickle cell disease.

I. INTRODUCTION
Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a group of hereditary disorders
caused by a mutation in the beta-globin gene, where glutamic
acid is replaced by valine at the sixth codon [1]. This mutation
causes red blood cells to polymerize into rigid, sickle-shaped
forms, impairing blood flow and leading to vaso-occlusion,
stroke, acute chest syndrome, pulmonary hypertension, and
leg ulcers [2].
SCD affects around 300,000 newborns annually, with over
80% of cases in Africa [3]. In Senegal, 10% of the population
carries the gene, and 1,700 babies are born with SCD yearly.
Projections estimate 14.2 million affected births by 2050 [4].
Affordable, reliable diagnostic tools are vital, particularly
in low-resource set-tings. Traditional blood smear analysis is
limited by skilled labor dependency, manual errors, delays,
and lack of advanced equipment [5]. Conventional methods
(e.g., Watershed, Hough Transform) fail with overlapping
cells and real-time integration, driving the adoption of deep
learning models (CNNs, Faster R-CNN, YOLO) [6]. Yet,
computational constraints hinder real-time deployment.
To address these challenges, we propose DREPADetect,
an optimized YOLOv8 model for sickle cell detection.
Enhanced for small object detection, it focuses on distinctive
features in blood smear images. Tested on datasets with varied
cell configurations (including overlaps), our model
outperforms YOLOv8 in accuracy and efficiency.
The key contributions of our study are following; (1)
Enhanced YOLOv8 for sickle cell detection, improving
accuracy in complex diagnostic cases; (2) Optimized
inference time, meeting real-time clinical constraints; (3)
Developed ‘SKDrepaData’, an annotated dataset (100×
magnification) to mitigate data scarcity; (4) Refined
overlapping cell detection, ensuring precise morphological
analysis; (5) Maintains high accuracy, even under imaging
artifacts, resolution inconsistencies, and lighting variations;
(6) Validated on multiple datasets (‘SKDrepaData’ and
‘Erythrocyte IBD1’), proving robustness in low-resource
environments.

II. RELATED WORK
Research on hematological disease diagnosis has
advanced significantly, with AI-based approaches enhancing
sickle cell detection through machine learning and deep
learning models. Early methods relied on image segmentation
and feature extraction but faced challenges in accuracy,
overlapping cell detection, and real-time feasibility [7].Traditional techniques, such as Otsu’s thresholding and
the Watershed algorithm, were used for feature extraction [8].
While effective, they suffered from high error rates with
overlapping cells and dependence on handcrafted features [9],
[10], [11]. Methods like edge detection (Sobel filter) and
circular Hough transform (CHT) struggled with noise and
complex cell structures[10], [11], prompting the shift to deep
learning [12].
CNN-based models have gained prominence. Bhatia et
al.[13] developed a custom CNN trained on 10,377 single-cell
images, outperforming LightGBM, Inception V3, ResNet, and
VGG. However, traditional CNNs remain computationally
intensive and require large datasets for optimal performance.
More recent works [14], [15], [16], [17] have leveraged
YOLO and R-CNN-based object detection architectures for
blood cell classification. John Paolo et al. [18] applied
YOLOv3 for sickle cell diagnosis, achieving a 50%
confidence score on a small dataset, highlighting YOLO’s
potential but also its dataset dependency. Karunasena et al.
[19] developed an R-CNN-based model, integrating CNN
feature extraction and SVM classification, reaching 90%
accuracy but with high computational costs unsuited for realtime practice on low-end CPUs.
Faster R-CNN has been applied to anemia-related cell
detection, with Haq et al. [20] achieving 91.83% accuracy for
RBCs, 97.90% for WBCs, and 88.36% for plate-lets.
However, it remains limited by inference delay, thus unsuited
for real-time clinical use.
Recent studies have optimized YOLO-based models for
sickle cell detection. Wang et al. [21] used YOLOv7 with
adaptive preprocessing, achieving mAP@50 scores of 80.2%
and 81.9%. Zeyu et al. [22] propose YOLO-BC, an improved
YOLOv8 variant, outperforming YOLOv8 with 80.9%
accuracy, mAP@50 of 90.1, F1-score of 85%, and Recall of
88.0%, demonstrating YOLOv8’s efficiency in hematological
analysis.
Despite advancements, existing models still face
challenges with overlapping cells, computational efficiency,
and real-time deployment. To address these limitations, we
introduce DREPADetect, an optimized YOLOv8-based
model tailored for sickle cell detection. By focusing on small
object detection, reducing computational costs, and improving
real-time inference, DREPADetect maintains high accuracy
while overcoming key drawbacks of prior models.
DREPADetect leverages elementary morphological
structures, extracting features based on minimal cell diameter
to effectively handle overlapping cells and feature extraction
challenges. It outperforms state-of-the-art models by
enhancing inference speed, enabling real-time decisionmaking, and addressing previous limitations making
DREPADetect an efficient, accurate, and real-time diagnostic
tool for SCD detection.
III. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
Existing sickle cell detection methods struggle with realtime deployment and over-lapping erythrocyte identification;
DREPADetect overcomes these challenges.
By exploiting the dimensional uniformity of erythrocytes
(~7–8 μm) and the fine details captured at high magnification
(×40, ×100), DREPADetect accurately identifies subtle
morphological features of sickle cells. High magnification
imaging reveals intricate cell structures, essential for precise
falciform shape detection.
Thus, the key challenge lies in designing a deep learning
model that focuses on these critical features while optimizing
both detection accuracy and processing speed. The proposed

A. DREPADetect Architecture
The DREPADetect architecture consists of three
components: Backbone, Neck, and Head. The Backbone, as
the core innovation for feature extraction, is the core
innovation of this study. In YOLOv8, the Backbone processes
640×640×3 input images through convolutional layers,
reducing spatial resolution while extracting features. Given
the dimensional consistency of sickle cells (~7–8μm) and the
irrelevance of large receptive fields for detecting individual
cells [23], our key improvement lies in removing unnecessary
detection blocks.
Rather than focusing on medium and large object
detection, as in YOLOv8, DREPDetect retains only blocks
optimized for small object detection, making it better suited
for erythrocyte identification. Specifically, Conv 0, Conv 1,
Conv 3, Conv 5, and C2f blocks are preserved, while Blocks
7-8, 10-12, and 16-21—which target medium and large
objects—are removed.
Additionally, the Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPPF)
module in the Backbone and the Contact and Upsample blocks
in the Neck are retained to enhance feature extraction, fusion,
and reconstruction, improving accuracy and efficiency. The
Neck refines extracted features, which the Head then classifies
and localizes. By streamlining YOLOv8, DREPADetect
achieves higher speed and precision, making it an efficient
sickle cell detection model suitable for resource-constrained
clinical environments.
IV. EXPERIMENTATION
A. Data Collection and Preprocessing
The experimental dataset consists of microscopic images
from 20 SCD patients, collected at three sites: the UMRED
Laboratory (University of Thiès), the Children's Hospital of
Diamniadio (Senegal), and the Cell Environment Laboratory
(Paris, France).
The ‘SKDrepaData’ [24] dataset, captured using an optical
microscope at 40× and 100× magnifications, consists of 619
images. Annotation was performed using Labelme [25] with
at least four hematology and biology experts, ensuring data
quality and reliability. Images were categorized into Normal
cells, Sickle cells, and other cell types, with their distinct
morphological features illustrated in Fig 2. To enhance model
generalization, variability and robustness, data augmentation
techniques such as flipping, 90° rotations, random cropping
(0–15%), and exposure adjustments (-20% to +20%) were
applied. This increased the number of images to 1,487, which
were split into 70% for training, 20% for validation, and 10%
for testing.
B. Experimental Setup and Evaluation Metrics
Experiments were conducted with PyTorch 2.5.1 on an
NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU (15,102 MiB VRAM) with CUDA
12.1 for accelerated computations. Training spanned 200
epochs, with early stopping (patience = 100) to prevent
overfitting while optimizing parameter exploration. A batch
size of 12 balanced gradient stability and memory efficiency.
The AdamW optimizer, known for its robust convergence,
was used with an initial learning rate of 0.001, a decay factor
of 0.1, and momentum of 0.9 to enhance stability.
Model performance was evaluated using Precision (P),
Recall (R), mean Average Precision (mAP), and FLOPs [26]
(equation 1). mAP combines precision and recall, with
mAP@50 reflecting precision at an IoU of 0.5, and mAP@50-
95 averaging precision across IoU thresholds (0.5–0.95, in
0.05 steps) for comprehensive evaluation. FLOPs quantify
DREPADetect’s computational complexity, measuring
floating point operations per second, providing a key indicator
of efficiency and processing speed.

DREPADetect’s performance is evaluated during training
and validation, highlighting the impact of its structural
optimizations. For a fair comparison, models were tested on
‘SKDrepaData’ using identical hyperparameters.

95) are detailed in Fig. 3 and TABLE I.

consistently outperforms YOLOv8 in Precision and mAP,

DREPADetect’s R = 87.6%, indicating that while
DREPADetect improves precision and detection accuracy,
YOLOv8 captures a broader range of sickle cells, albeit with
a higher false positive rate. The comparative validation table
(Table I.) further confirms benefits of DREPADetect
optimizations over the baseline.

B. Benchmarking Computational Efficiency
The experimental results on computational efficiency,
measured in GFLOPs, high-light DREPADetect’s superior
resource management. The model significantly reduces
computational complexity, requiring only 118.7 GFLOPs,
compared to 164.8 GFLOPs for YOLOv8. Additionally,
DREPADetect operates with 16,250,067 parameters, whereas
YOLOv8 has 43,608,921, reflecting a 62.7% reduction in
model size and a 28% decrease in computational demand for
image processing.
The proposed optimizations also reduce the model’s
depth, with DREPADetect using 150 layers compared to 268
in YOLOv8. Additionally, image processing time improves
by at least 4 milliseconds, with DREPADetect achieving
14.1ms per im-age, compared to 18.3ms for YOLOv8. These
results confirm DREPADetect’s efficiency, ensuring high
performance while reducing computational costs, making it
better suited for real-time clinical applications.
C. Discussion
The experimental results confirm that DREPADetect,
through architectural optimization, outperforms state-of-theart models. Its structural innovations, specifically tailored to
this study, yielded highly satisfactory results across all
evaluation stages.
During training, as shown in Fig 3., DREPADetect
demonstrates a faster and more efficient learning process than
YOLOv8. This improvement stems from the drastic reduction
of non-essential layers, allowing the model to focus on crucial
detection features, particularly cell size. Additionally, data
preprocessing techniques—including augmentation—
enhanced generalization capability, with these optimizations
also reflected in validation performance.
In the validation phase, DREPADetect's optimized
backbone and neck, designed for small object detection,
enable the model to filter out irrelevant background elements
and focus directly on target features. However, as shown in
TABLE I, DREPA-Detect recorded a lower Recall (87.6%),
likely due to the limited training data avail-able in this study.
Recognizing the critical role of data quantity, we have made
our annotated dataset publicly available to support further
research in the scientific community.

In cell identification and detection, DREPADetect
enhances computational efficiency by 28% compared to
YOLOv8, reducing operations per second while achieving
22.9% faster image processing. The model’s simplified
structure—150 layers versus 268 in YOLOv8—significantly
boosts execution speed. These computational optimizations
make DREPADetect highly suitable for clinical applications,
where real-time processing and efficiency are essential.
D. Experimental Comparative of Models on the
SKDrepaData Dataset
DREPADetect demonstrates superior efficiency in sickle
cell detection compared to recent models (TABLE II). While
YOLOv7-tiny focuses on hypochromic microcytes (IDA) and
AttriDet offers explainability but low performance (mAP =
28.2%), both lack clinical validation. YOLO-BC achieves
high precision (80.9% P, 88.9% R, mAP@50 = 90.1%) but is
computationally intensive, hindering real-time use on low-end
CPUs. YOLOv5-BS, optimized for speed, struggles with
overlapping cells. Two stage models like R-CNN and Faster
R-CNN provide >90% precision but suffer from slow
inference and poor generalization. Custom Deep CNNs are
noise-sensitive, and CST-YOLO, despite high recall (92.7%),
is computationally demanding and untested on specific
diseases.
DREPADetect strikes an optimal balance, achieving
87.6% precision, 87.6% recall, and 90.3% mAP@50. Tailored
for sickle cell detection, it ensures real-time feasibility and
suitability for low resource clinical settings. This study also
introduces a new expert annotated dataset, enhancing
reproducibility and clinical validation.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
We developed DREPADetect, an optimized YOLOv8-
based method for blood cell detection and counting. Tailored
for clinical use, it detects small-sized cells (normal, sickle, and
others) while ensuring real-time performance for practical
applications. Experimental results demonstrate that
DREPADetect outperforms YOLOv8, achieving a precision
of 87.6%, mAP@50 of 90.3%, and mAP@50-95 of 64.6%.
Additionally, the model significantly reduces computational
complexity, requiring 118.7 GFLOPs and 150 layers,
compared to 164.8 GFLOPs and 268 layers for YOLOv8,
thereby improving speed and efficiency while maintaining
superior performance. A comparative analysis with other
models, such as recent YOLO derivatives propositions and
Faster R-CNN, confirms that DREPADetect delivers better
overall performance in red blood cell detection, surpassing
these models in terms of precision, recall, and mAP.
Our future research will focus on: (1) Developing an
integrated DREPADetect based system for sickle cell
detection and counting, ensuring accessible diagnostics in
remote areas of Senegal. (2) Expanding and releasing a public
sickle cell dataset to mitigate data scarcity and advance
hematological research. (3) Exploring federated learning to
facilitate secure collaboration across medical centers,
improving model performance and clinical adoption.
